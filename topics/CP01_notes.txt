PIPING FUNDAMENTALS:

PURPOSE AND FUNCTION: Transports fluids and fluid/solid mixtures between equipment. Design must account for temperature, pressure, flow rate, chemical/physical properties, contamination, and shock loads.

DESIGN FACTORS:
• Temperature affects material properties, expansion, and thermal stress
• Pressure determines wall thickness, material selection, support design
• Flow rate/velocity affects erosion, corrosion, and pressure drop
• Shock loads (water hammer, steam hammer, thermal shock, seismic) require robust design
• Changes in direction require proper support and restraint

MATERIAL SELECTION:
• Low-carbon steel: economical, prone to rust/corrosion
• Low-alloy steel: high-temperature applications, improved wear/fatigue/corrosion resistance
• Stainless steel: corrosion resistant, minimizes corrosion product buildup
• Copper alloys: excellent corrosion resistance, good conductivity
• Nickel alloys: high temperature/pressure applications, high cost
• Plastic: corrosion resistant, lightweight, limited temperature range

PIPE SUPPORT SYSTEMS:
• Rigid restraints: fixed, prevent movement
• Spring hangers: support vertical gravity loading, allow thermal movement
• Snubbers: limit seismic motion while allowing thermal growth

THERMAL EFFECTS:
• Thermal loops/bends: accommodate thermal expansion
• Expansion joints: allow controlled linear expansion
• Insulation: controls heat loss/gain and protects personnel

[Note ID: 2]
FIVE PRINCIPAL VALVE FUNCTIONS:

1. STARTING/STOPPING FLOW: Isolation to completely stop or start fluid flow (gate valves, ball valves, plug valves)

2. THROTTLING FLOW: Control flow rate without stopping completely, requires throttling-capable design (globe valves, needle valves)

3. CONTROLLING DIRECTION OF FLOW: Routes fluid to different paths (directional control valves, check valves)

4. REGULATING PRESSURE: Maintains desired system pressure (pressure regulators, pressure-reducing valves)

5. RELIEVING PRESSURE: Protection against overpressure (relief valves, safety valves)

BASIC VALVE CONSTRUCTION:
• Body: contains internal flow path, seats, and internal parts
• Bonnet: upper section that seals the body
• Disk: moves to open/close or throttle flow
• Seat rings: provide seal surface for the disk
• Stem: connects disk to actuator/handwheel
• Backseat: allows packing adjustment without system depressurization
• Packing/stuffing box: compression seal prevents stem leakage

STEM DESIGNS:
• Rising stem: stem moves upward when valve opens (visual position indicator)
• Non-rising stem: stem does not move (position not visually obvious)

[Note ID: 3]
PRESSURE FUNDAMENTALS:

DEFINITION: Pressure = Force / Area (P = F/A). Units: psi, Pa, bar, atm. Normal atmospheric pressure = 14.7 psia = 101.3 kPa.

HYDROSTATIC PRESSURE: Pressure from weight of fluid column above reference point. Formula: P = rho x g x h. Increases with depth. Acts in all directions equally.

ABSOLUTE PRESSURE: Measured from perfect vacuum (zero reference). Units: psia. Thermodynamic calculations require absolute pressure. Example: 10 psig = 24.7 psia.

GAGE PRESSURE (PSIG): Pressure above atmospheric. Formula: Pgage = Pabsolute - Patm. Common pressure gauge indication.

VACUUM PRESSURE: Pressure below atmospheric. Formula: Pvac = Patm - Pabs. Used in condenser and pump suction applications.

DIFFERENTIAL PRESSURE (PSID): Difference between two locations. Used for filter drop, valve throttling, level measurement.

PASCAL'S LAW: Pressure applied to confined fluid is transmitted undiminished throughout in all directions. F1/A1 = F2/A2.

HYDRAULIC PRESS APPLICATION: Input force on small piston creates pressure throughout system. Pressure acts on larger piston producing larger output force. Work In = Work Out: F1 x d1 = F2 x d2.

[Note ID: 4]
THERMAL BINDING:

CAUSE: Valve body cools faster than disk during system cooldown. Differential thermal contraction binds seat against disk. Can make valve impossible to reopen until reheated.

PREVENTION:
• Periodically crack valve open and re-shut during cooldown
• Use proper closing torque (avoid over-torquing)
• Allow gradual cooling when possible

PRESSURE LOCKING:

CAUSE: Liquid trapped in valve bonnet pressurizes as it heats. Pressure buildup prevents valve opening. Common in high-temperature systems during startup.

PREVENTION:
• Provide relief or vent path on valve bonnet
• Equalize/reduce pressure buildup
• Design relief path to lower trapped pressure

OPERATING EXPERIENCE LESSONS:

1. Valve Handling: Never use wrench on valve handwheel for extra leverage. Can overstress actuator gearing/casing. Has caused catastrophic actuator failure.

2. Manual Operation Verification: Reaching hard stop on handwheel does NOT confirm full travel. Verify full designed number of turns. Position limit switch not reliable sole indicator.

3. Manual Override Precautions: Manually operating past designed open limit can slip position limit switch. Requires leak rate/stroke time retest after manual operation. Document any manual override operations.

KEY MAINTENANCE PRACTICES:
• Regular inspection for leakage
• Packing maintenance using backseat feature
• Periodic exercise to prevent stiction
• Monitor position indicators for consistency
• Track valve operation history

[Note ID: 5]
ACTUATOR FAILURE POSITIONS:
• Fail Open: opens on loss of power/control (safety for emergency drainage)
• Fail Closed: closes on loss of power (safety for isolation)
• Fail As Is: remains in current position (most Motor Operated Valves)
• Fail Safe: the position required to protect the system/equipment

MANUAL ACTUATORS:
• Handwheel or lever: direct mechanical operation
• Reach rods: extends control distance
• Gearing: provides mechanical advantage for higher torque

ELECTRIC ACTUATORS:
• Motor Operated Valves (MOVs): Fail As Is, gear reduction for torque, declutch lever for manual/motor switching
• Solenoid Operated Valves: Two-position (on/off only), spring placement determines fail position

PNEUMATIC ACTUATORS:
• Gas cylinder, piston, diaphragm, or vane designs
• Quarter-turn designs for butterfly/plug valves
• Spring action determines fail position on air loss

HYDRAULIC ACTUATORS:
• Piston-driven for high-force applications
• Spring action determines fail position on pressure loss

VALVE POSITION VERIFICATION METHODS:
• Light indication: Red = Open, Green = Closed, Both lit = Mid-position/Throttled
• Travel indication: Rising stem position, local mechanical pointer
• Actual verification: Visual stem observation, handwheel turn count, quarter-turn valve lever position

HUMAN ERROR PREVENTION:
• Verify all local/remote indications before and after manipulation
• Follow procedures and require independent verification
• Confirm valves and instrumentation are labeled correctly
