TWO SYSTEMS OF MEASUREMENT:

AMERICAN ENGINEERING SYSTEM (ENGLISH ENGINEERS SYSTEM):
• Force: pounds force (lbf), Mass: pounds mass (lbm)
• Temperature: Fahrenheit (°F), Pressure: psi
• Conversion: Work in Btu = (Force in lbf x Distance in ft) / 778

INTERNATIONAL SYSTEM (SI/METRIC):
• Force: Newtons (N), Mass: kilograms (kg)
• Temperature: Celsius (°C) or Kelvin (K)
• Pressure: Pascal (Pa), kilopascal (kPa), or bar
• Simpler conversion based on powers of 10

UNIT CONVERSION PROCESS:
1. Identify given units and desired units
2. Write conversion factor: given units in denominator, desired units in numerator
3. Multiply original value by conversion factor
4. Cancel units and solve

COMMON CONVERSION FACTORS:
• 1 lbm = 0.4536 kg, 1 psi = 6.895 kPa
• K = C + 273.15, R = F + 459.67

[Note ID: 7]
RELATIVE SCALES (reference point based):

Fahrenheit (°F):
• Freezing point of water: 32°F
• Boiling point of water: 212°F
• Interval: 180 degrees between freeze/boil
• Used in American Engineering System

Celsius (°C):
• Freezing point of water: 0°C
• Boiling point of water: 100°C
• Interval: 100 degrees between freeze/boil
• Formula: °C = (°F - 32) / 1.8

ABSOLUTE SCALES (zero = no molecular motion):

Rankine (°R):
• Corresponds to Fahrenheit scale
• Absolute zero: 0°R = -459.67°F
• Formula: °R = °F + 459.67
• Maintains 1°F per 1°R interval

Kelvin (K):
• Corresponds to Celsius scale
• Absolute zero: 0 K = -273.15°C
• Formula: K = °C + 273.15
• Maintains 1°C per 1 K interval

WHY ABSOLUTE TEMPERATURE REQUIRED: All thermodynamic equations require absolute temperature. Relative scales produce wrong results in calculations.

[Note ID: 8]
DEFINITION OF A SYSTEM: A quantity of matter or space separated from surroundings by a boundary. Everything outside is the surroundings.

THREE SYSTEM TYPES:

ISOLATED SYSTEM: No mass transfer, no energy transfer. Theoretical ideal; real systems always exchange something.

CLOSED SYSTEM: No mass transfer, energy transfer (heat/work) may occur. Most common for thermodynamic analysis. Used for fixed mass of gas in cylinder, sealed reactor vessel, closed piping sections.

OPEN SYSTEM (CONTROL VOLUME): Mass transfer across boundary (inlet/outlet flows), energy transfer may occur. Most realistic for practical equipment. Used for turbines, compressors, boilers, pumps, condensers, pipes with flow.

WORKING FLUID: The fluid within a system that stores or transfers energy. Must be able to absorb and reject heat. Examples: steam, refrigerant, gas, water.

SYSTEM PROPERTIES:

INTENSIVE PROPERTIES (independent of mass): Pressure, Temperature, Density, Specific volume, Velocity, Height, Enthalpy, Entropy

EXTENSIVE PROPERTIES (dependent on mass): Volume, Mass, Weight, Internal energy, Enthalpy, Entropy. Total = mass x specific property.

SPECIFIC PROPERTIES: Intensive properties derived from extensive ones. Calculated as: specific property = extensive property / mass.
