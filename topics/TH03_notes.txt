STATE OF A SUBSTANCE: Condition at specific moment, uniquely defined by two independent intensive properties. Once two properties known, all others can be determined via tables or equations.

PHASE: Describes molecular structure. Four phases: Solid (fixed shape/volume), Liquid (fixed volume, takes container shape), Vapor/Gas (no fixed shape/volume), Plasma (ionized gas).

PHASE CHANGES:
• Melting: Solid to Liquid (absorbed heat at constant pressure)
• Solidification: Liquid to Solid (rejected heat)
• Vaporization: Liquid to Gas (absorbed heat)
• Condensation: Gas to Liquid (rejected heat)
• Sublimation: Solid to Gas directly (skips liquid)

PHASE DIAGRAM (PRESSURE-TEMPERATURE):
• Fusion line: Solid-Liquid boundary
• Vaporization line: Liquid-Gas boundary
• Sublimation line: Solid-Gas boundary
• Triple Point: Where all three phases coexist in equilibrium
• Critical Point: Highest T and P where liquid and gas distinguish

PHASE DIAGRAM (PRESSURE-VOLUME): Vapor dome region where liquid and vapor coexist. Left of dome: subcooled liquid. Right of dome: superheated vapor.

[Note ID: 13]
FIVE STATES OF WATER:

1. SUBCOOLED LIQUID: Below saturation temperature. Resistant to vaporization. Nearly incompressible.
2. SATURATED LIQUID: At saturation temperature. On verge of boiling. All liquid molecules condensed.
3. WET VAPOR: Mixture of saturated liquid and vapor. Both phases coexist at same temperature.
4. SATURATED VAPOR: 100% vapor at saturation. Last drop of liquid just evaporated. Critical for turbine inlet quality.
5. SUPERHEATED VAPOR: Above saturation temperature. All liquid converted. Most flexible state.

HEAT TRANSFER DURING PHASE CHANGE:

SENSIBLE HEAT: Heat addition/removal that changes TEMPERATURE. No phase change. Observable temperature change.

LATENT HEAT: Heat addition/removal that causes PHASE CHANGE at constant temperature. All energy goes into changing molecular structure/bonds.

LATENT HEAT OF VAPORIZATION (LHV/Hfg): Energy needed to convert saturated liquid to saturated vapor. Same energy released when vapor condenses. Decreases as pressure increases.

LATENT HEAT OF CONDENSATION: Opposite of vaporization. Energy released when steam condenses to liquid.

STEAM QUALITY (X) AND MOISTURE CONTENT (M):
• Quality: Fraction of vapor in wet mixture. Range 0 (liquid) to 1.0 (vapor).
• Moisture content: Fraction of liquid. M = 1 - X.
• Void fraction: Volumetric fraction of vapor (not mass fraction).

IMPORTANCE OF STEAM QUALITY: High quality required at turbine inlet (>95-99%). Low quality causes blade erosion and corrosion.

[Note ID: 14]
IDEAL GAS BEHAVIOR:

CHARLES' LAW (constant pressure): Gas volume directly proportional to absolute temperature. V/T = constant. V1/T1 = V2/T2.

BOYLE'S LAW (constant temperature): Gas volume inversely proportional to absolute pressure. P x V = constant. P1V1 = P2V2.

COMBINED/IDEAL GAS LAW: Combines Charles and Boyle. Equation: PV/T = nR or PV = nRT. Specific form: Pv = RT for one lbm of gas.

APPLICATIONS: Air and gas systems, low pressures, high temperatures. Does NOT work for steam near saturation.

WHY IDEAL GAS LAW FAILS FOR STEAM: Steam behavior MORE COMPLEX. Intermolecular forces significant. Proximity to saturation violates ideal assumptions. PV/T NOT constant.

STEAM TABLES: Experimentally-measured properties of water and steam. Account for complex non-ideal behavior. Tabulated for common pressures/temperatures.

TABLE TYPES:
• Saturated steam tables: Indexed by saturation temperature or pressure. Properties at saturation point.
• Superheated steam tables: Indexed by pressure and temperature. For steam ABOVE saturation.
• Compressed liquid tables: Properties below saturation temperature.

DEGREES OF SUBCOOLING: Difference between saturation and actual liquid temperature. Formula: Degrees subcooling = Tsat - Tactual.

DEGREES OF SUPERHEAT: Difference between actual and saturation temperature. Formula: Degrees superheat = Tactual - Tsat.
