FIVE ESSENTIAL ELEMENTS OF A THERMODYNAMIC CYCLE:

1. A Working Fluid: Substance operating through cycle (typically steam or gas). Must absorb and reject heat. Properties change through processes.

2. An Engine: Device converting heat energy to mechanical work. Examples: turbines, pistons, compressors. Efficiency determines heat conversion.

3. A Heat Source: Supplies thermal energy to working fluid. Examples: boiler, furnace, solar radiation. Temperature affects cycle efficiency.

4. A Heat Sink: Absorbs waste heat rejected by cycle. Examples: condenser, cooling tower, atmosphere. Lower temperature improves efficiency.

5. A Device to Move Working Fluid: Pump or compressor circulates working fluid. Requires work input. Necessary for continuous cycle operation.

CYCLE EFFICIENCY: Cycle efficiency = net work out / heat added = (QA - QR) / QA. The Second Law requires some heat always be rejected.

[Note ID: 20]
THE CARNOT CYCLE: Theoretical maximum efficiency standard.

FOUR PROCESSES:

1. ISOTHERMAL EXPANSION (1 to 2): Temperature constant. Heat absorbed from heat source. Pressure decreases, volume increases. Work output by system.

2. ADIABATIC EXPANSION (2 to 3): No heat transfer. Temperature and pressure decrease. Working fluid expands against turbine. Maximum work extracted.

3. ISOTHERMAL COMPRESSION (3 to 4): Temperature constant. Heat rejected to heat sink. Volume decreases, pressure increases. Work input to system.

4. ADIABATIC COMPRESSION (4 to 1): No heat transfer. Temperature and pressure increase. Returns to initial state. Work input to system.

CARNOT EFFICIENCY FORMULA: eta = 1 - (Tcold / Thot). Where Tcold and Thot are absolute temperatures (Kelvin). Depends ONLY on temperature ratio. Independent of working fluid type.

KEY PRINCIPLE: Highest possible efficiency for any engine operating between given heat source and heat sink. Real cycles always fall short due to friction and non-reversible processes. Carnot sets theoretical maximum.

[Note ID: 21]
THE RANKINE CYCLE:

OVERVIEW: More realistic steam cycle used in actual power plants. Operates with four main processes using steam as working fluid.

FOUR PROCESSES:

1. PUMP COMPRESSION (1→2): Adiabatic compression. Increases liquid pressure. Pump requires work input (negative work output).

2. HEAT ADDITION (2→3): Constant-pressure (isobaric) process. Boiler/steam generator raises feedwater enthalpy. Converts subcooled liquid to saturated or superheated steam.

3. TURBINE EXPANSION (3→4): Adiabatic expansion. Decreases pressure and enthalpy. Produces useful work output. Turbine extracts energy from steam.

4. HEAT REJECTION (4→1): Constant-pressure (isobaric) process. Condenser removes latent heat of condensation. Converts exhausted steam to liquid for pump return.

NET WORK: Wnet = Wturbine - Wpump. Pump work typically 1-5% of turbine work.

VS. CARNOT CYCLE:
• More realistic: uses isobars instead of isotherms
• All pressure changes occur at pump & turbine points
• Easier to analyze using steam tables
• Lower efficiency than Carnot but achievable in practice

EFFICIENCY:
• Efficiency computed from enthalpies using steam tables
• Real turbines and pumps add entropy (friction losses)
• Efficiency lower than ideal due to irreversibilities

HOW TO INCREASE CYCLE EFFICIENCY:

TWO PRIMARY METHODS BASED ON CARNOT:

1. INCREASE HEAT SOURCE TEMPERATURE (Thot):
• More energy input from higher temperature source
• Larger temperature difference with heat sink
• Efficiency gain proportional to temperature increase
• Example: Superheating steam in boiler
• Practical limit: Material strength at high temperatures
• Method: Increase boiler temperature/pressure

2. DECREASE HEAT SINK TEMPERATURE (Tcold):
• Larger temperature difference with heat source
• More work extracted per unit of heat input
• Example: Improving condenser cooling (vacuum)
• Practical limit: Ambient temperature (cannot go below)
• Deep vacuum in condenser maximizes this effect
• Method: Improve condenser cooling efficiency

ADDITIONAL RANKINE CYCLE IMPROVEMENTS:

1. SUPERHEATING THE STEAM:
• Increases efficiency (more net work than added heat rejected)
• Raises average temperature of heat addition
• Reduces moisture content in low-pressure turbine

2. FEEDWATER HEATING:
• Extraction steam preheats feedwater
• Increases efficiency: less heat needed from heat source
• Typical improvement: 1-3% per extraction point
• Modern plants often have 3-8 feedwater heaters

3. MOISTURE SEPARATOR REHEATER (MSR):
• Protects turbine blades from erosion
• Minor effect on cycle efficiency
• Real purpose: drying/superheating steam to LP turbine

4. HIGHER STEAM PRESSURE:
• Increases efficiency: lower entropy in and out
• More turbine work available
• Limited by material properties

5. HIGHER STEAM QUALITY:
• More enthalpy available for net work
• Minimum quality >95-99% to prevent blade damage
• Condenser vacuum improvements help maintain quality

OPERATIONAL FOCUS:
• Efficiency best at full load
• Minimize auxiliaries and leaks
• Fix steam leaks and air leaks
• Regular maintenance and cleaning
• Proper operator technique and experience

CONDENSER VACUUM: CRITICAL FACTOR IN EFFICIENCY

WHAT IS CONDENSER VACUUM:
• Pressure inside condenser lower than atmospheric
• Expressed as absolute pressure (psia) or inches of mercury
• Example: 1 psia absolute = about 29 inches mercury vacuum
• Lower absolute pressure = higher vacuum = better efficiency

WHY LOWER PRESSURE INCREASES EFFICIENCY:

1. INCREASED EXPANSION RATIO:
• Pressure drop across turbine: from boiler to condenser
• Lower condenser pressure = larger expansion ratio
• More work extracted per unit of steam
• Turbine does more work with same steam flow

2. MORE WORK OUTPUT:
• At atmospheric condenser: less work extracted
• At vacuum condenser: more work extracted from same steam
• Extra work directly increases efficiency

3. FORMULA RELATIONSHIP:
• Carnot efficiency: eta = 1 - (Tcold/Thot)
• Lower pressure = lower temperature of condenser
• Larger T_hot/T_cold ratio = higher efficiency

IMPROVING CONDENSER VACUUM:

1. COOLING WATER FLOW:
• More/cooler cooling water improves heat rejection
• Lowers steam condensation temperature
• Maintains lower absolute pressure

2. AIR REMOVAL:
• Vacuum pump removes air from condenser
• Air reduces effective pressure
• Regular maintenance keeps pump effective

3. CONDENSER TUBE CLEANING:
• Fouling reduces heat transfer
• Clean tubes allow better cooling
• Regular cleaning improves vacuum

4. TEMPERATURE MANAGEMENT:
• Cooling water temperature affects vacuum
• Seasonal variations impact performance
• Cold water = better vacuum = higher efficiency

PRACTICAL IMPACT:
• Each inch mercury improvement in vacuum: increases efficiency ~0.5-1%
• Increases power output
• Major focus area for optimization
• Cost-effective improvement strategy
• Monitored continuously

HEAT RATE: MEASURE OF PLANT PERFORMANCE

DEFINITION: Heat Rate = Energy Input (BTU) / Electrical Output (kW-hr). Units: BTU/kW-hr (typical range 7,000-10,000).

EXAMPLE: Plant with heat rate 8,000 BTU/kW-hr: To produce 1 kW-hr electricity requires 8,000 BTU fuel input. Waste: 4,588 BTU (1 kW-hr = 3,412 BTU).

RELATIONSHIP TO EFFICIENCY:
• Inverse relationship: Lower heat rate = Higher efficiency
• Efficiency (%) = 3,412 / Heat Rate x 100
• Example: 3,412 / 8,000 x 100 = 42.65% efficiency

WHY IT MATTERS:
• Economic: 1% improvement = significant fuel cost savings
• Environmental: Lower heat rate = less fuel burned, reduced emissions
• Operational: Real-time measure of plant performance, indicates maintenance needs

IMPROVING HEAT RATE:
• Condenser vacuum improvements
• Feedwater heating optimization
• Moisture separator reheater tuning
• Reducing auxiliary power consumption
• Regular maintenance and cleaning
• Operator skill and experience

INDUSTRY BENCHMARKS:
• Best-in-class plants: ~8,200 BTU/kW-hr
• Average plants: ~9,000-9,500 BTU/kW-hr
• Older plants: >10,000 BTU/kW-hr
